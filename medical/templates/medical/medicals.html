Useful Tips for Data Entry

  • Age: Enter your current age. Accurate age helps in risk assessment for age-related diseases.
  • Gender: Selecting the correct gender can impact the accuracy of disease risk assessments.
  • WBC (White Blood Cell count): Check your latest blood test results for accuracy. Values outside the normal range might indicate an infection or inflammation.
  • RBC (Red Blood Cell count): Low RBC may indicate anemia. Provide the most recent test result.
  • HGB (Hemoglobin): Important for transporting oxygen. Low levels may indicate anemia.
  • HCT (Hematocrit): Measures concentration of red blood cells in blood. Critical for diagnosing anemia and other conditions.
  • MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume): Helps to classify types of anemia. Low or high MCV can indicate different types of anemia.
  • MCH (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin): Reflects the average amount of hemoglobin per red cell. Important for diagnosing anemia types.
  • MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration): Helps evaluate the concentration of hemoglobin in red blood cells.
  • RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width): High RDW levels can indicate significant variations in red cell size, useful in diagnosing anemia.
  • PLT (Platelet count): Essential for blood clotting. Low platelet count can increase bleeding risk.
  • MPV (Mean Platelet Volume): Provides information on platelet size; larger platelets may be more active.
  • Neutrophils: A type of white blood cell important for fighting infections.
  • Lymphocytes: High levels can indicate viral infections; low levels could be due to severe infection or immunodeficiency.
  • Monocytes: High levels can indicate chronic inflammation or infections.
  • Eosinophils: High levels might indicate allergic reactions or parasitic infections.
  • Basophils: Though typically a small portion of white blood cells, high levels can suggest chronic inflammation or allergic response.
  • Glucose: Test on an empty stomach for the most accurate glucose level reading.
  • Creatinine: Important for kidney function assessment. High values may indicate a need for increased water intake before testing.
  • ALT and AST: Important liver enzymes. Avoid alcohol and fatty foods before testing to avoid false elevations.
  • Cholesterol: Record the latest measurement. High levels may necessitate dietary changes.
  • Continents: Select the continent you reside in to consider regional health risks
  • Bilirubin: High levels can indicate liver problems or jaundice. Watch for yellowing of the skin and eyes as a physical sign.
  • Albumin: Reflects nutritional status. Low levels could indicate malnutrition or liver issues.
  • Phosphorus: Plays a key role in body energy processes; important to keep balanced, especially for kidney health.
  • Magnesium: Low levels can cause muscle spasms and general fatigue. Important for heart and nerve functions.
  • Sodium: Vital for fluid balance. Monitor closely if you have issues with blood pressure or kidney function.
  • Potassium: Essential for muscle function and heart health. Abnormal levels can have significant health impacts.
  • Chloride: Works with sodium to help keep your fluid and electrolyte balance. Important for digestion and maintaining pH balance.
